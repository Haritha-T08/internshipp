Artificial intelligence

How AI Works



Modern AI is built on a few core concepts that allow machines to "think" without being explicitly programmed for every scenario: Machine Learning (ML): This is the engine of AI. It involves feeding a computer vast amounts of data so it can identify patterns. For example, by looking at millions of photos of cats, the system learns what "cat-ness" looks like. Neural Networks: Inspired by the human brain, these are layers of algorithms that process information. Deep Learning uses many layers of these networks to solve complex problems, like recognizing speech or driving a car. Large Language Models (LLMs): These are the "talkative" AIs. They predict the next most likely word in a sequence based on the massive amount of text they’ve read, allowing them to write essays, code, or chat.

Types of AI



AI is generally categorized by its capabilities: 1. Narrow AI (Weak AI) This is what we use today. These systems are experts at one specific task. Examples: FaceID on your phone, Netflix recommendations, or AlphaGo (the AI that beats world champions at the game of Go). Limitation: A chess-playing AI cannot tell you the weather; it only knows chess. 2. General AI (Strong AI / AGI) This is a theoretical AI that would possess the ability to understand, learn, and apply knowledge across any domain, just like a human. We haven't reached this stage yet.

Why Is AI Everywhere Now?



AI isn't new—the concept dates back to the 1950s—but three things recently collided to make it explode: Massive Data: The internet provides the "textbooks" AI needs to learn. Computing Power: Modern chips (GPUs) can perform the billions of calculations required for deep learning. Algorithmic Breakthroughs: Researchers developed new ways for models to "pay attention" to context, leading to the generative AI boom we see today.
